The "Unhackable" Console:
A War Still Ongoing

I. The Beginning 2006

Microsoft's legacy of console, once called the unhackable, ignited a lost war against its own so-called secure "Hypervisor". Before the RGH/JTAG, the modders' scene was a battleground of innovation and determination—and still is. Enthusiasts and exploiters have relentlessly pursued the goal of unlocking the full potential of the 360, just to prove Microsoft wrong, in addition to upholding the real meaning of owning a console, where the power is solely in the hands of us owners.

In late 2006, hacker Felix Domke (aka “tmbinc”) discovered a hypervisor bug that allowed unsigned code execution on an Xbox 360 running an older kernel (version 4532). This became known as the “King Kong” exploit because it was demonstrated using a modified copy of Peter Jackson’s King Kong. At the 23rd Chaos Communication Congress in December 2006, Domke showcased the exploit by booting the game and then showing the Xbox 360, Mac OS X, and Linux logos with the text “Coming soon…” – a teaser that Linux could run on the 360.

At first we only had basic workarounds that got removed whenever we rebooted. The KK Exploit, where it all began, leveraged a bug in the handling of a specific system call within the hypervisor, the console's core security layer. By literally tricking it into running unsigned code by using the game King Kong's save files just to run homebrew applications, it was a small step, but a significant one, marking the beginning of a new era for the 360 modding community.

II. 2010 Getting Engineers' Support

The war had just begun. Enthusiast engineers got on board, marking the discovery of the first physical permanent exploit. Introducing the first exploit that marked the beginning of the end for Microsoft's "Unhackable" Console: The KAMIKAZE!

It was all talks at first and theoretical concepts, till the first attempt was successful. The Kamikaze exploit, named after the Japanese World War II pilots, was a literal drilled hole in the DVD rom chip that aimed to disable all essential parts sending signals to the hypervisor, consequently disabling all DRM checks.
